My placement journey — what I wish someone had told me in 4th semester
Three years ago I was exactly where most of you are now — overwhelmed, unsure of what companies expect, and genuinely scared I'd miss the bus. I want to share a practical timeline that actually worked for me, including the mistakes I made along the way that I never see people talk about honestly.

The biggest mistake I made was not starting projects early enough. I spent two months grinding LeetCode problems before realizing that companies at our level (Infosys, TCS, Wipro) actually care more about your project portfolio and communication than your competitive programming rating. If I could go back, I'd start with one solid full-stack project in 4th semester, then layer DSA practice on top.

Why every CS student should learn system design basics before final year
I've been conducting mock interviews for juniors lately, and the one consistent gap I keep seeing is system design thinking. Even for entry-level positions, companies like Amazon and Google now include at least one "design-oriented" round.

You don't need to master distributed systems right away. Start with the fundamentals — load balancing, caching strategies, database sharding concepts, and basic API design. I recommend starting with "Designing Data-Intensive Applications" by Martin Kleppmann. It's dense, but even reading the first five chapters will put you ahead of 90% of fresh graduates. Pair this with building a small project that involves at least a REST API, a database, and some basic caching layer.
#system-design #interviews #tech #learning

Reminder: How to choose your final year project wisely
Every year I see students scramble to find a project topic in their 7th semester. Let me save you some stress with a framework I've seen work well over the years.

First, pick a domain you're genuinely curious about — ML, web development, IoT, whatever interests you. Then, look for a real problem to solve rather than duplicating existing projects you find on GitHub. Talk to local businesses, NGOs, or even other departments in college. A placement panel will always prefer a project that solves a real-world problem over a technically impressive but directionless one. Finally, document everything from day one. Your project report matters more than most students realize.
#projects #final-year #guidance #academic

Just completed my first open source contribution — here's how I did it
I've been wanting to contribute to open source for over a year, but always felt intimidated. Last week I finally made my first pull request to a real project and it got merged! Here's what helped me get over the fear.

I started by looking at repositories tagged with "good first issue" on GitHub. I found a documentation bug in a popular JavaScript testing library — nothing fancy, just fixing a code example that had an outdated API call. The entire process from fork to merged PR took about 3 hours. The maintainers were incredibly supportive. If you're hesitant like I was, just start with docs or test improvements. You don't need to rewrite core logic to be a contributor.

The AI landscape is shifting fast — here's what I'd focus on if I were a student right now
Working at Google has given me a front-row seat to how quickly the AI/ML space is evolving. If you're a student wondering whether to invest time in AI or stick to traditional software engineering, here's my honest take.

Learn both, but don't spread yourself thin. Get solid at one programming language (Python is the safest bet), understand data structures well, and then pick ONE area of AI — whether it's NLP, computer vision, or reinforcement learning — and go deep. The graduates who stand out aren't the ones who've done ten shallow Coursera courses; they're the ones who can explain their one project in intricate detail and demonstrate genuine curiosity about the field.
