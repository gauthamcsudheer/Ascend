Having just finished placements last semester, here's the honest breakdown no one gave me clearly in Sem 4.

Priority order:

  • Sem 5: Pick one solid project. Build something end-to-end — doesn't matter what, matters that you can explain every decision you made. Simultaneously start LeetCode Easy — 20-30 minutes a day is enough.
  • Sem 6: Ramp up DSA to Medium difficulty. Start reading about system design concepts (no need for formal prep yet). Revise your project and make sure you can talk about it for 20 minutes straight.
  • Sem 7: Mock interviews, system design for product companies, competitive coding if targeting FAANG-adjacent roles. This is when intensity should peak.

On CGPA — it matters as a cutoff, not as a ranking metric. Most companies visiting Rajagiri use 6.5 or 7.0 as a floor. Once you clear that, your technical performance is what separates you. Don't sacrifice project quality chasing CGPA points.

Resources that actually helped me: NeetCode for DSA, Alex Xu's System Design Interview book, and honestly — Ascend itself for finding seniors whose journeys matched mine.

Adding a perspective from someone who's been in the industry for 2 years now — the thing that surprised me most after joining was how little LeetCode actually matters on the day-to-day. What matters is that you can read unfamiliar code, debug systematically, and communicate your thought process clearly.

So my advice: treat DSA as a vehicle to learn how to think algorithmically, not as a thing to memorize. And do at least one internship before your final placement season, even if it's unpaid or a small startup. The experience of working in a real codebase is worth more than 200 LeetCode problems.

From a faculty perspective, I'd add one thing the other answers haven't emphasised: communication skills matter more than most students expect. Every year we see technically strong students stumble in interviews because they struggle to articulate their thought process.

Practice explaining your code out loud. Do mock interviews — even informal ones with a friend. The placement process at companies visiting Rajagiri almost always includes a communication-heavy round, especially for roles beyond pure coding.
